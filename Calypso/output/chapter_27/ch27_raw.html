All Alabama real estate salesperson and broker licenses are registered to a specific real estate office. Any individual wishing to be licensed as a broker in Alabama must apply for a broker's license using an AREC-designated form which specifies the real estate office under which the individual is registered.

The individual must also meet the education, experience, and personal requirements for becoming an Alabama broker. Issuance of broker licenses General requirements. Criminal background. Just as when applying for a salesperson license, if the applicant has been convicted of a felony or a crime involving moral turpitude, he or she may file an "Application for Determination of Licensing Eligibility" form with the Commission. Salesperson experience. The broker applicant also must have been licensed as an active real estate salesperson in any state for at least 24 months before applying for a broker license. The 24 months of licensure must have occurred within the 36 months prior to applying for the Alabama broker license. Proof of this licensure must be submitted to the Commission along with the broker license application.

Coursework and state examination. Additionally, the broker applicant must complete a 60-hour AREC-approved broker pre-license real estate course. After the applicant has met all requirements, he or she must apply to take the national and state broker licensure examination, pay the examination fee, and pass the examination with a score of at least 70%. When the applicant has met all requirements, including passing the examination, he or she will apply for the broker license. Once the broker license is issued, the broker's salesperson's license automatically terminates. The salesperson's license certificate is to be returned to the Commission for the broker's license to be issued. An applicant for a broker's license must maintain a place of business. Associate broker status. According to Alabama license law, all brokers must be registered to a real estate business and under the business's qualifying broker. Once registered, the broker becomes an associate broker with that qualifying broker. By definition, an associate broker is any broker other than a qualifying broker, made so by his or her relationship with the qualifying broker.

TRUST FUNDS

Purpose Trust funds defined Qualifying broker responsibilities Disbursement of trust funds Trust fund management Trust fund scams Escrow account reconciliation

A trust fund account, also known as an escrow account, is a bank account set up to hold funds belonging to someone other than the real estate broker owning the account. The account is set up by the brokerage company's qualifying broker at a federally insured banking institution that is located in Alabama. The funds are held until such a time that a specific event, such as a real estate closing, takes place.

Purpose When a property is being purchased, the buyer provides earnest money to the broker to be held until the purchase closes. These earnest funds are to be deposited into a bank account other than the broker's business or personal account. This trust fund account keeps the client's money separate from the broker's money. This ensures that any money the client needs to use for the real estate transaction is in a secure account that is accessible only by the client or the broker. As long as the money is held in a trust account in a bank insured by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), each client's funds are insured up to $250,000.

Trust funds defined Alabama license law and AREC rules require a real estate licensee to deposit and account for all funds belonging to or being held for others at all times. These funds include earnest money, security deposits, and all other funds related to a contract for the sale or lease of property or a property management agreement. They are money or other items of value that the broker receives on behalf of an individual (usually the property buyer) to be held for the benefit of others in relation to any acts for which a real estate license is required.

The funds may be U.S. currency (cash), checks, notes, other recognized forms of money, or other items of value. Cash is to be deposited immediately. Checks may be held for a specific length of time before being deposited if the purchase offer designates the length of time or the event that must occur prior to the check being deposited. If the offer does not state a specific time for the check to be deposited, it is to be deposited when the offer becomes a contract.

Qualifying broker responsibilities Alabama laws and rules also require that the individual responsible for holding trust funds is to be the qualifying broker.

The qualifying broker is responsible for depositing the funds belonging to others to be held in trust unless he or she is expressly relieved of the responsibility in writing. Thus, the qualifying broker is the trustee for the account. If a purchase contract involves more than one qualifying broker, any of the qualifying brokers involved in the sale may deposit and hold the funds related to the purchase. Funds being held in relation to a purchase contract, a lease, or a property management agreement are to be deposited and held by the qualifying broker who is providing these services to the property owner. If the services are provided by a secondary or successor broker, the first broker is to provide a complete accounting of the funds to the successor qualifying broker and is to transfer the funds to the successor broker. The qualifying broker who is currently providing the services to the owner is responsible to the public and to the Commission for the funds. At the request of the Commission, the current qualifying broker must immediately account for any trust funds he or she holds. The qualifying broker is to be a customer of the bank or financial institution where the trust account is held and is to be one of the persons with authority to deposit and withdraw funds from the account. He or she may write or make checks as necessary on all trust accounts set up on behalf of the company. Disbursement of trust funds The qualifying broker holding the trust funds must disburse the funds to the appropriate party or parties within 7 days of the consummation of the transaction for which the funds were being held. In other words, when the purchase is completed and closed, the funds must be disbursed within 7 days of that closing date.

If the transaction does not close or there is disagreement regarding who should receive the funds, the qualifying broker must not disburse any of the funds unless there is a court order or until there is a written agreement signed by all parties. Alternatively, the broker may deposit the funds with the appropriate court under the rules of interpleader or any other lawful procedure that is applicable. The rules of interpleader remove the qualifying broker from the disbursement process and make the disagreeing parties litigate the disbursement between themselves. The qualifying broker becomes the stakeholder, and the disagreeing parties become the claimants. The stakeholder then would deposit the funds under dispute with the court, and the court would decide who is legally entitled to the funds. Thus, the qualifying broker is released from any liability regarding disbursement of the funds.

Alabama license law has defined procedures which real estate professionals must follow in relation to trust fund accounts. The law has also assigned penalties for failing to perform any of these acts. The law requires the licensee to properly account for or remit money belonging to others that comes into his or her possession. Consequently, licensees must be able to correctly determine the amount of trust funds they are holding. Failure to do so is a violation of the law. The law also requires the broker to deposit and account for all funds belonging to others or being held for others in a federally insured account in a financial institution located in Alabama. Failure to do so is a violation of the law.

No commingling. Further, the licensee is required to maintain trust accounts separate from his or her personal or business accounts to hold funds belonging to others. The trust account may hold funds belonging to multiple clients but not funds belonging to the broker. Failure to separate the broker's funds from the funds belonging to others is called commingling and is a violation of Alabama law. However, the AREC does allow the broker to keep some personal funds in a trust account to cover bank service charges or check fees. The qualifying broker can lose his or her license if the broker uses any money from the trust account for his own personal or business use. The broker is not only responsible for his or her own actions, but also for the actions of the salespersons and brokers which the qualifying broker supervises. Thus, the qualifying broker may be held responsible for any commingling, embezzling, and conversion of his or her client's money committed by associate brokers or salespersons. Violations and discipline. If a licensee violates any of these provisions of the law pertaining to trust funds and the violation is reported in writing to the AREC, the Commission or its staff may investigate the actions and records of the licensee. Just as with any other violation or complaint, the Commission may issue subpoenas and hear testimony of witnesses and require trust fund records and documents to be produced during the course of the investigation. If the Commission finds probable cause as a result of the investigation, the Commission will file a formal complaint and hold a hearing on the complaint. If the hearing determines the licensee is guilty of an alleged trust fund violation, the Commission will impose any or all of the following penalties for each act for which the licensee is found guilty:  require the licensee to complete continuing education courses in addition to the existing CE requirements  suspend or revoke the licensee's license  impose a fine of at least $100 but not more than $5,000  suspend or revoke the license and impose a fine  reprimand the licensee

Trust fund scams In this age of technology, it is common for funds to be transferred and deposited into bank accounts by way of electronic transfers. However, it is also unfortunately common for electronic hackers and scammers to use the same transfer methods to commit email and wire fraud and obtain funds belonging to others.

The scammer typically hacks into an email account or impersonates the email of a party involved in a real estate transaction. Once the scammer has the email of the involved party, the scammer transmits false wiring information for the transfer of funds from the real estate transaction. The funds then are wired to the scammer's bank account and then immediately transferred to a bank out of the country, making it impossible to recover the funds. Typically, neither the scammer's bank or the victim's bank will reimburse the funds. ESTIMATED CLOSING STATEMENT

Purpose for the estimate Estimated closing expenditures Disclosure to consumer

A real estate closing is the transfer of the property title from the seller to the buyer in accordance with the sales contract. However, there are numerous costs associated with the closing that can become quite complex. The costs involved are determined by the sales contract, local tradition and custom, type of financing, and local, state, and federal laws.

The closing statement. A closing statement is an accounting document used in the real estate transaction closing that details the final costs related to a transfer of ownership of property. It is also known as a settlement statement.

While the closing statement presents the final settlement costs, buyers and sellers need to know the approximate transaction costs at the time an offer to purchase the property is made. Therefore, the law requires that an estimated closing statement be produced for both the buyer and the seller at the time an offer or counteroffer is made. The estimate approximates settlement costs so that each party will be duly informed and prepared. All parties to the transaction should compare the estimated closing statement to the final closing statement to find and correct potential errors.

Providing an estimated closing statement to the buyer and seller also helps protect against mortgage fraud by eliminating the potential for unexpected and/or unexplained costs charged by the bank or mortgage company at the actual closing. Purpose for the estimate Any licensee who represents a residential buyer and obtains a written purchase offer must prepare and provide an estimated closing statement to the buyer. The statement must contain the most accurate and complete estimates of all costs that are the buyer's responsibility at the time the transaction closes. The statement must itemize not only which costs the buyer will pay but also the estimated amount of each cost. The licensee must provide the statement to the buyer at the time the buyer signs the offer to purchase.

Any licensee who represents a residential seller and presents a written purchase offer to such seller must also prepare and provide an estimated closing statement to the seller. The statement provided to the seller must show which costs are the seller's responsibilities and approximately how much each of those costs will be. This statement is to be provided to the seller at the time the offer is presented. By signing the estimated and the actual closing statements, both the buyer and the seller are acknowledging that they received the statements. TILA, RESPA and TRID. ADVERTISING

Advertising with an interest Signage Team Advertising

When advertising real estate, licensees must be honest. They are prohibited from making false promises that could potentially influence, persuade, or induce a consumer to enter into a contract. They are also prohibited from flagrant misrepresentation in any form of advertising. Publishing any advertisement that deceives the public or is likely to deceive the public is against the law. All advertisements must identify the advertiser as a licensed broker or salesperson. Advertising with an interest Alabama license law exempts property owners from licensing requirements when the owners are managing their own property or engaging in a real estate transaction involving their own property. In other words, a property owner can manage, lease, or sell his or her own property without needing to be licensed as a real estate salesperson or broker. This also means the property owner can advertise his or her own property for lease or sale without needing to be licensed.

Alabama license law also allows any property owner who becomes a licensed broker or salesperson to advertise his or her own property. However, while the non-licensed owner is exempt from the license laws, the licensed owner must abide by all provisions of the law and AREC rules in any real estate transaction. This includes transactions in which the licensee is the property owner or the spouse, child, or parent of the property owner. Thus, the licensee must also comply with all applicable laws when advertising his or her own property for lease or sale. Further, if the licensee is advertising his or her own property or property belonging to a member of the licensee's immediate family, the licensee must provide prior written disclosure of his or her interest in the property to all parties to the transaction. The licensee must also disclose that he or she is a licensed real estate salesperson or broker. The law states the disclosure must be "timely" without giving a definitive time frame. If the licensee is seeking to purchase an interest in a property being sold by his or her brokerage company, the licensee must disclose his or her intention to the property seller or the seller's agent. The licensee must inform the seller that he or she is a licensed real estate salesperson or broker and that he or she is the intended buyer of the property.

Signage Real estate licensees have the legal right to advertise property for sale, lease, or transfer using signs that are affixed to or located on the property itself. Alabama does not allow a municipal corporation or local government to impose a fee or tax on the signs, whether the signs are located on or near the property.

However, local, county and city governments are allowed to set and enforce reasonable restrictions on the size, height, and placement of signs on private

The restrictions include the manner of erection and posting of signs, interference or obstructions caused by the signs, removal and disposal of the signs, etc. Team Advertising Real estate teams have specific advertising rules that apply only to them. While the law has granted AREC the ability to adopt rules regarding the words that may and may not be used in team names and how teams may market and advertise their services, the law does proscribe a few of its own requirements. In team advertisements, the name of at least one team member and the name of the real estate company where the team members are licensed must both be included in the ad. Either “team” or “group” must be included in the team name. In addition, the qualifying broker must give written authorization to allow the team to include its name in any advertisements.

RECORDKEEPING

Both estimated closing statements and the actual closing statements must be retained in the broker's files. The buyer's agent should retain the estimated and actual closing statements signed by the buyer in his or her qualifying broker's files, while the seller's agent retains the statements signed by the seller. Neither licensee is required to retain statements signed by both the buyer and the seller. Nor is it necessary for cooperating brokers to maintain both the buyer's statement and the seller's statement. Although the law does not specify a time frame for retention of closing statements, as with any documents or records related to a real estate transaction, the statements should be retained for at least 3 years.

STATEMENT

Purpose ● the closing statement details the costs related to the transfer of property ownership.

● the estimated closing statement approximates closing costs so each party is informed and prepared.

● an estimated closing statement helps protect the consumer against mortgage fraud

● licensees must provide an estimated closing statement to any buyer who submits a written purchase offer for a single family residential property, with the statement including each cost the buyer will pay and the estimated amount of each cost; the statement is due at the time the buyer signs the purchase offer.

● licensees must provide an estimated closing statement to any seller at the time a written purchase offer is presented, with the statement including each cost the seller will pay and the estimated amount of each cost.

● licensees must provide an actual closing statement to both the buyer and the seller when the sale closes, with the statement showing all receipts and disbursements.

● the buyer and seller acknowledge receiving the statements by signing both the estimated and the actual closing statements. Estimated closing expenditures ● a debit to the seller is a credit to the buyer and vice versa.
